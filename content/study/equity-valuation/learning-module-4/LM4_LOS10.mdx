## Intuition Building

A persistent challenge in relative valuation is comparing the Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratios of companies growing at vastly different speeds. A high-growth technology stock will virtually always trade at a higher P/E than a mature utility. To bridge this gap, practitioners developed the **P/E-to-Growth (PEG) ratio**, which attempts to calculate the "cost" of a stock per single percentage point of expected growth. While Wall Street relies heavily on the PEG ratio as a quick heuristic to spot undervalued growth stocks, the CFA curriculum emphasizes its significant theoretical flaws. 

## Core Concept Teaching

**1. The Mechanics of the PEG Ratio:**
The PEG ratio standardizes the P/E multiple by dividing it by the expected earnings growth rate (expressed as a whole integer, not a decimal). 
- **The Heuristic:** All else being equal, lower PEG ratios are definitively more attractive than higher PEG ratios. A widely cited (though overly simplistic) rule of thumb on Wall Street is that a stock is "fairly valued" when its P/E equals its growth rate (a PEG exactly equal to 1.0). A PEG below 1.0 flags potential undervaluation.

**2. The Three Fatal Theoretical Flaws of the PEG Ratio:**
While mathematically convenient, the PEG ratio breaks several foundational rules of equity valuation dictating how multiples actually behave.

1.  **It assumes a linear relationship between P/E and growth:** The PEG formula assumes that if growth doubles, the justified P/E should exactly double. However, the Gordon Growth Model ($\frac{1 - b}{r - g}$) mathematically proves the relationship between P/E and growth is absolutely *not* linear—it is exponential.
2.  **It completely ignores differences in risk:** The P/E multiple is heavily driven by the required rate of return (risk). Two companies might have identical P/E ratios and identical growth rates ( yielding identical PEG ratios), but if Company A operates with massive financial leverage and Company B is debt-free, Company A is actually significantly overvalued relative to its high risk. The PEG ratio is utterly blind to this.
3.  **It ignores the duration of growth:** The PEG ratio typically divides the current P/E by a short-term (e.g., 3-to-5 year) growth forecast. A company expected to grow at 15% for only 3 years will generate the exact same PEG ratio as a company expected to grow at 15% for 20 years. The latter is obviously vastly more valuable.

**Analyst Interpretation:**
Top-tier analysts never use the PEG ratio in isolation to make a hard buy/sell decision. They use it as a rapid filtering mechanism across a sector. If an analyst screens 50 enterprise software stocks, all with relatively similar risk profiles and growth durations, the stocks floating to the top with the lowest PEG ratios become the prime candidates for grueling, full-scale DCF modeling.

**Exam Trap:**
The CFA Institute loves testing the calculation of the PEG ratio specifically to check if you remember to use the growth rate as a *whole number integer* rather than a decimal. If a stock trades at a 20x P/E and has a 10% expected growth rate, the PEG is $20 / 10 = 2.0x$. If you accidentally divide by 0.10, you will calculate a PEG of 200, which will always be a distractor option designed to trap you. 

## Ground-Up Framework

**PEG Ratio** = `(P/E Ratio) / (Expected Earnings Growth Rate × 100)`

*Note: The growth rate in the denominator is input as a whole number. A 12% growth rate is entered as 12, not 0.12.*

## Examples / Applications

**Scenario: Comparing High-Growth Telecoms via PEG**
An analyst is evaluating two telecommunications companies. 
- **CenturyLink:** Trailing P/E = 8.9x, Expected 5-Year EPS Growth = 8.5%
- **Charter Communications:** Trailing P/E = 30.3x, Expected 5-Year EPS Growth = 45.3%

Which stock is relatively more undervalued adjusting for growth?

*Step 1: Calculate CenturyLink's PEG*
`PEG = 8.9 / 8.5` = **1.04**

*Step 2: Calculate Charter's PEG*
`PEG = 30.3 / 45.3` = **0.67**

*Direct Source Anchoring:* Matching the curriculum's Example 11, despite Charter trading at an astronomical 30.3x P/E multiple (more than triple CenturyLink's), its explosive expected 45% growth rate results in a vastly superior PEG ratio (0.67 vs 1.04). Assuming risk levels are comparable, Charter is actually the "cheaper" stock per unit of expected growth.

## Summary / Key Takeaways

*   **Entering $g$ as a Decimal:** The absolute most common error when calculating the PEG ratio is dividing the P/E by a decimal. If the growth rate is 15%, you must divide the P/E by 15. If a stock has a P/E of 30x, the PEG is 2.0x. Dividing 30 by 0.15 yields 200, an absurd figure that will instantly ruin a comparative analysis on the exam.
*   **The Negative Growth Trap:** If a company is expected to shrink (negative expected earnings growth), the PEG ratio generates a negative number, rendering the entire metric mathematically meaningless. The PEG ratio structurally cannot evaluate highly mature companies drifting into terminal decline.
*   **The Risk Blindspot:** If an exam vignette asks *why* an analyst should avoid blindly buying the stock with the lowest PEG ratio across two wildly different sectors, the primary answer is "Ignoring Risk." A highly regulated, safe utility and a volatile biotech startup have fundamentally different Costs of Equity ($r$); the PEG ratio ignores this disparity entirely.
