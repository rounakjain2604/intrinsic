## Intuition Building

In a multistage Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model, an analyst forecasts specific cash flows (or dividends/earnings) year-by-year for an explicit horizon (e.g., the next 5 years). Because a company is assumed to be a going concern that lives forever, the analyst must calculate a **Terminal Value** at the end of that explicit horizon to capture the value of all cash flows extending into infinity. This Learning Outcome Statement (LOS) details how analysts use both forecasted fundamentals and relative comparables (specifically price multiples) to calculate this critical Terminal Value.

## Core Concept Teaching

The terminal value typically represents 50% to 80% of a stock's total calculated DCF value, making its accurate estimation paramount. Analysts rely on two primary methods to derive a terminal price multiple:

**1. Terminal Price Multiple Based on Forecasted Fundamentals**
This method relies on the Gordon Growth Model to establish the value at the end of the high-growth stage (Year $n$).
- **The Process:** By dividing the Gordon Growth formula $V_n = \frac{D_{n+1}}{r - g}$ by earnings, the analyst mathematically derives a justified terminal P/E multiple.
- **The Pro:** It is theoretically pure. It ensures the terminal multiple is strictly governed by the company's assumed long-term sustainable growth rate ($g$) and required return ($r$).
- **The Con:** The Gordon Growth Model is notoriously hyper-sensitive. A 1% tweak to the long-term growth assumption can swing the terminal multiple—and thus the final valuation—wildly.

**2. Terminal Price Multiple Based on Comparables (Market Multiples)**
Rather than relying on intense mathematical forecasting, the analyst simply applies a benchmark market multiple (e.g., the industry average P/E, or the company's own historical average P/E) to the forecasted earnings at the end of the explicit horizon.
- **The Process:** The analyst forecasts earnings in Year $n$, and multiplies it by the selected benchmark multiple.
- **The Pro:** The valuation is deeply grounded in actual, observable market data rather than academic theory.
- **The Con (The Fatal Flaw):** A DCF is supposed to calculate *intrinsic* value independent of market moods. By injecting a market-derived multiple into the end of a DCF, the analyst infects the intrinsic valuation with market sentiment. If the industry is currently experiencing an irrational bubble (trading at 45x P/E instead of a historical 15x), the terminal value will be artificially inflated, defeating the purpose of the DCF model entirely.

**Analyst Interpretation:**
In elite investment banking and equity research, analysts almost always calculate terminal value using *both* methods. They will calculate the terminal value using the Gordon Growth (Perpetuity Growth) method, and then calculate an implied exit multiple to see if it makes sense. If the fundamental math implies an exit P/E of 25x, but the industry historically has never traded above 12x, the analyst knows their growth ($g$) assumption is far too aggressive and needs revision.

**Exam Trap:**
The CFA Institute frequently tests the "Mismatch Trap." If using a **Trailing** Terminal P/E, you must multiply it by the earnings of the *current* terminal year ($E_n$). If using a **Forward** Terminal P/E, you must multiply it by the projected earnings of the *next* year ($E_{n+1}$). Mixing these up will result in an incorrect terminal value calculation that perfectly matches a distractor answer option.

## Ground-Up Framework

**Method 1: Using a Benchmark Multiple (Comparables)**
$V_n = \text{Benchmark Trailing P/E} \times E_n$
*Or:*
$V_n = \text{Benchmark Forward P/E} \times E_{n+1}$

**Method 2: Using the Gordon Growth Model (Fundamentals)**
$V_n = \frac{D_n \times (1 + g)}{r - g} = \frac{D_{n+1}}{r - g}$

*(Note: Once $V_n$ is calculated in the future, it must be discounted back to Present Value ($V_0$) using $(1 + r)^n$.)*

## Examples / Applications

**Scenario: Estimating Terminal Value for an Oil Explorer**
An analyst is modeling an explicitly forecasted 3-year high-growth phase for an oil exploration firm. 
- The required rate of return ($r$) is 10%.
- Earnings in Year 3 ($E_3$) are projected to be $3.00. 
- The firm's mature dividend payout ratio is expected to be 45%.
- The industry average ROE is 13%.
- The industry average trailing P/E is 14.3x.

*Calculation 1: Terminal Value via Comparables*
Using the industry average P/E as the benchmark multiple:
$V_3 = \text{Benchmark P/E} \times E_3$
$V_3 = 14.3 \times \$3.00$ = **$42.90**

*Calculation 2: Terminal Value via Gordon Growth Model Fundamentals*
First, calculate the sustainable growth rate ($g$):
$g = \text{Retention Rate} (b) \times ROE = (1 - 0.45) \times 0.13 = 0.55 \times 0.13 = 0.0715$ (or 7.15%)
Next, calculate the dividend in Year 3 ($D_3$):
$D_3 = \$3.00 \times 0.45 = \$1.35$
Finally, apply the Gordon Growth formula to find $V_3$:
$V_3 = \frac{D_3 \times (1 + g)}{r - g} = \frac{\$1.35 \times (1 + 0.0715)}{0.10 - 0.0715} = \frac{\$1.4465}{0.0285}$ = **$50.75**

*Direct Source Anchoring:* Following the curriculum's Example 17, we see the discrepancy. The fundamental Gordon Growth model generates a terminal value 18% higher ($50.75 vs $42.90) than the comparables method. The analyst must investigate: Is the market currently undervaluing oil stocks (depressing the 14.3x peer multiple)? Or is the assumption that this firm can sustain a 7.15% growth rate into infinity impossibly optimistic?

## Summary / Key Takeaways

*   **The Mismatch Trap:** You cannot multiply a *Forward* P/E by $E_n$, nor can you multiply a *Trailing* P/E by $E_{n+1}$. The curriculum frequently gives you both earnings estimates and both multiple types to see if your methodology is internally consistent.
*   **The Double-Counting Error:** When calculating the intrinsic value of the firm at Year 0 ($V_0$), you must discount both the explicit cash flow received in Year $n$ AND the massive Terminal Value generated in Year $n$. Do not forget the final year's standalone cash flow before throwing the Terminal Value into the discounting math.
*   **The Mispricing Contagion:** A major essay question or conceptual trap involves asking what the inherent mathematical flaw of a price multiple terminal value strategy is. The answer: If the market is currently overvaluing the chosen peer group, using their inflated P/E as your terminal multiple infects your otherwise purely fundamental DCF with irrational market valuation errors.
