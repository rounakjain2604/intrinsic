## Intuition Building

While the **method of comparables** evaluates price multiples against peer benchmarks, the **method based on forecasted fundamentals** links a price multiple directly to a discounted cash flow (DCF) model—typically the Gordon Growth Model. Understanding the fundamental drivers underlying these multiples—namely profitability, growth, and risk—allows analysts to calculate a mathematically **justified price multiple** and explain precisely why one company legitimately commands a structurally higher or lower valuation than its peers. This LOS breaks down the fundamental factors influencing Price-to-Book (P/B), Price-to-Sales (P/S), Price-to-Cash Flow (P/CF), and Dividend Yield (D/Y).

## Core Concept Teaching

To intuitively grasp valuation, you must understand what drives each multiple mathematically derived from a constant-growth DCF model:

**1. Price-to-Book (P/B) Fundamentals:**
- **The Driver:** The single most crucial driver of a justified P/B is **Return on Equity (ROE)**.
- **The Relationship:** P/B is positively related to ROE and expected growth ($g$), and inversely related to the required return on equity ($r$). 
- **Key Insight:** A firm earning exactly its required return ($ROE = r$) destroys no value and creates no value; it should theoretically trade at a justified P/B of 1.0x (assuming $g=0$). A company generating an ROE greater than its cost of equity commands a P/B > 1.0x, representing active franchise value creation.

**2. Price-to-Sales (P/S) Fundamentals:**
- **The Driver:** The dominant fundamental driver of the P/S ratio is the **Net Profit Margin** (Net Income / Sales).
- **The Relationship:** P/S is positively related to profit margins and earnings growth ($g$), and inversely related to the required return ($r$).
- **Key Insight:** Two firms with identical massive sales volumes can trade at vastly different P/S ratios. A company with a 20% net profit margin mathematically justifies a significantly higher P/S multiple than a peer with a razor-thin 2% net margin.

**3. Price-to-Cash Flow (P/CF) Fundamentals:**
- **The Driver:** Predictable, high-growth cash generation.
- **The Relationship:** The justified P/CF is positively related to the expected growth rate of future cash flows and inversely related to the cost of equity ($r$).
- **Key Insight:** All else equal, if a company structurally lowers its cost of capital or signals higher long-term cash flow growth, its justifiable P/CF multiple expands.

**4. Dividend Yield (D/Y) Fundamentals:**
- **The Driver:** Yield is an inverse valuation metric (Price is in the denominator rather than the numerator), meaning its fundamental relationships flip relative to the other multiples.
- **The Relationship:** Dividend yield is **positively** related to the required rate of return ($r$) and **inversely** (negatively) related to the expected growth rate in dividends ($g$).
- **Key Insight:** A skyrocketing dividend yield often acts as a warning siren. Rather than signaling a "deep value" bargain, an excessively high yield typically indicates the market is demanding a massive required return due to high fundamental risk, or projecting deeply negative future dividend growth.

**Analyst Interpretation:**
Amateur analysts look at two competing telecom stocks, observe Company A trading at 1.5x Book Value and Company B trading at 3.0x, and conclude Company A is a "screaming buy." Professional analysts immediately look at ROE. If Company B generates a sustainable ROE of 25% while Company A generates an ROE of 6% (below its cost of capital), Company B might actually be the cheaper stock strictly based on its forecasted fundamentals. Understanding the intrinsic drivers prevents analysts from falling into superficial "value traps."

**Exam Trap:**
The CFA Institute frequently tests the mathematical interplay between P/B, ROE, and Cost of Equity ($r$). Memorize the parity rules: 
- If **$ROE > r$**, Justified **P/B > 1.0x**. 
- If **$ROE = r$**, Justified **P/B = 1.0x**. 
- If **$ROE < r$**, Justified **P/B < 1.0x** (The business structurally bleeds value and trades at a justifiable discount to its accounting equity). 
For Dividend Yield, the trap lies in its inversion: remember that a *higher* fundamental growth rate ($g$) mathematically *lowers* the justified yield, which runs counter-intuitive to how standard P/E multiples operate.

## Ground-Up Framework

The formulas dictating Justified Multiples are algebraic rearrangements of the Gordon Growth Model ($V_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}$):

- **Justified P/B Formula:** 
  **$\frac{P_0}{B_0} = \frac{ROE - g}{r - g}$**
- **Justified P/S Formula:** 
  **$\frac{P_0}{S_0} = (Profit Margin) \times (Payout Ratio) \times \frac{1 + g}{r - g}$**
  *(or simplified based on trailing data)*
- **Justified Dividend Yield Formula:** 
  **$\frac{D_0}{P_0} = \frac{r - g}{1 + g}$**

## Examples / Applications

**Scenario: Connecting ROE to the Justified P/B Multiple**
An equity analyst is evaluating a regional bank with a Cost of Equity ($r$) of 10% and a sustainable, mature long-term growth rate ($g$) of 4%.
Through intensive fundamental analysis, the analyst forecasts the bank will sustain an ROE of 13%.

Let's compute the Justified Price-to-Book multiple:
- **Justified P/B** $= \frac{0.13 - 0.04}{0.10 - 0.04} = \frac{0.09}{0.06}$ = **1.50x**

*Direct Source Anchoring:* As illustrated in the curriculum's algebraic derivations, the numerator of the P/B formula ($ROE - g$) dictates the premium over book computationally. If the bank's stock is currently trading in the market at a P/B of 1.1x, the analyst generates an actionable recommendation: the stock is fundamentally undervalued, because the current market price implies a significantly lower multiple than the firm's 13% intrinsic profitability (ROE) mathematically justifies.

## Summary / Key Takeaways

*   **The Zero-Growth Paradox:** If the curriculum presents a company with stagnant, 0% growth ($g = 0$), the Justified P/B formula simplifies perfectly to `ROE / r`. This is a classic, rapid-fire exam calculation that tests your algebraic flexibility.
*   **Profit Margins Drive Sales Multiples:** Never blindly compare P/S ratios across varying industries. Enterprise software companies command 10.0x P/S ratios, while high-volume supermarkets command 0.3x P/S ratios—not because the supermarkets are deeply undervalued, but because their fundamental baseline Net Profit Margins are in the single digits.
*   **The High Yield Value Trap:** Value investors blindly chasing the highest Dividend Yield often purchase structural failures. The formula `D0 / P0 = (r - g) / (1 + g)` mathematically proves that a massive percentage yield mechanically occurs when risk skyrockets (driving up required return, $r$) or when the market projects permanent negative future dividend growth ($g$).
