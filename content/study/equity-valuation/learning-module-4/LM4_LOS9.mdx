## Intuition Building

In the method of forecasted fundamentals, a price multiple is not determined by looking at peers, but by mathematically deriving what a company's multiple *should* be based on its intrinsic growth, profitability, and risk profile. By rearranging the Gordon Growth Model (Constant Growth Dividend Discount Model), analysts can calculate a **justified multiple**. If a stock's actual trading multiple in the market is lower than this justified multiple, the stock is theoretically undervalued. This LOS covers the specific mathematical formulas to calculate the Justified P/E, P/B, and P/S ratios.

## Core Concept Teaching

Every justified multiple begins with the foundational Gordon Growth Model:
$V_0 = \frac{D_1}{r - g}$ 
*(Where $D_1$ is next year's dividend, $r$ is the required return, and $g$ is the constant growth rate).*

By strategically dividing both sides of this equation by different fundamental denominators ($E_1$, $E_0$, $B_0$, $S_0$), the model transforms into distinct price multiples.

**1. Justified Price-to-Earnings (P/E)**
The math differs slightly depending on whether you are calculating a *forward* (leading) P/E or a *trailing* P/E.
- The dividend payout ratio is mathematically represented as $(1 - b)$, where $b$ is the retention rate.
- **Forward P/E:** You divide the model by *next* year's earnings ($E_1$). The formula simplifies cleanly to just the payout ratio divided by $(r - g)$.
- **Trailing P/E:** You divide the model by *this* year's earnings ($E_0$). Because the current price is driven by next year's dividend ($D_1$), which equals $D_0 \times (1 + g)$, the $(1 + g)$ term must remain in the numerator.

**2. Justified Price-to-Book (P/B)**
By substituting the Sustainable Growth Rate formula ($g = ROE \times b$) into the Gordon Growth Model and dividing both sides by Book Value ($B_0$), the $(1 - b)$ payout ratio algebraically transforms into $(ROE - g)$.
- **Key Insight:** If a firm earns exactly what its investors demand ($ROE = r$), the numerator $(ROE - g)$ equals the denominator $(r - g)$, and the justified P/B is exactly 1.0x. This is known as "par value" in equity valuation.

**3. Justified Price-to-Sales (P/S)**
To derive P/S, we take the Justified Trailing P/E formula and simply multiply it by the Net Profit Margin ($E_0 / S_0$).
- **Key Insight:** This mathematical derivation proves that, holding growth and risk constant, a company's justified sales multiple is entirely dictated by its net profit margin.

**Analyst Interpretation:**
These formulas force analysts to align their assumptions. If a junior analyst forecasts a massive 30% ROE for a mature industrial firm but models a low 5% growth rate and an 8% cost of equity, the formula will spit out a Justified P/B of `(0.30 - 0.05) / (0.08 - 0.05) = 8.3x`. A senior portfolio manager will immediately recognize that an 8x book value for a mature industrial is absurd, proving the 30% ROE forecast is likely impossibly optimistic. The mathematics act as a lie detector for the assumptions.

**Exam Trap:**
The most common mistake on test day is confusing the Trailing P/E formula with the Forward P/E formula. If the question gives you $E_0$ and asks for the trailing justified multiple, you **must** multiply the numerator by $(1 + g)$. If the question provides $E_1$ and asks for the forward justified multiple, do **not** use the $(1 + g)$ term. The examiners will relentlessly test this exact nuance.

## Ground-Up Framework

These formulas must be memorized flawlessly for the exam:

**1. Justified Forward P/E:**
$\frac{P_0}{E_1} = \frac{Payout\_Ratio}{r - g} = \frac{1 - b}{r - g}$

**2. Justified Trailing P/E:**
$\frac{P_0}{E_0} = \frac{Payout\_Ratio \times (1 + g)}{r - g} = \frac{D_0 / E_0 \times (1 + g)}{r - g}$

**3. Justified Price-to-Book (P/B):**
$\frac{P_0}{B_0} = \frac{ROE - g}{r - g}$

**4. Justified Trailing Price-to-Sales (P/S):**
$\frac{P_0}{S_0} = \text{Profit Margin} \times \frac{Payout\_Ratio \times (1 + g)}{r - g} = \frac{E_0}{S_0} \times \frac{(1 - b)(1 + g)}{r - g}$

## Examples / Applications

**Scenario: Calculating the Trifecta of Justified Multiples**
An analyst is evaluating Zenlandia Robotics using the following forecasted fundamentals:
- Required Rate of Return ($r$): 10.0%
- Expected Constant Growth Rate ($g$): 4.0%
- Return on Equity ($ROE$): 14.5%
- Dividend Payout Ratio ($1 - b$): 45.0%
- Net Profit Margin ($E_0 / S_0$): 12.0%

*Calculation 1: Justified Forward P/E*
`P0 / E1 = 0.45 / (0.10 - 0.04) = 0.45 / 0.06` = **7.50x**

*Calculation 2: Justified Trailing P/E*
`P0 / E0 = [0.45 × (1 + 0.04)] / (0.10 - 0.04) = 0.468 / 0.06` = **7.80x**

*Calculation 3: Justified P/B*
`P0 / B0 = (0.145 - 0.04) / (0.10 - 0.04) = 0.105 / 0.06` = **1.75x**

*Calculation 4: Justified Trailing P/S*
`P0 / S0 = 0.12 × 7.80` (using the Trailing P/E calculated above) = **0.936x**

If Zenlandia Robotics is currently trading in the market at a trailing P/E of 9.0x, a P/B of 2.2x, and a P/S of 1.5x, the analyst can confidently conclude the stock is broadly **overvalued** across all metrics, as the actual market multiples significantly exceed the multiples justified by the company's underlying fundamentals.

## Summary / Key Takeaways

*   **The Zero-Growth ($g=0$) Simplification:** If a vignette specifies a "no-growth" or mature, stagnant firm, the formulas dramatically simplify. `P/E = 1 / r`, `P/B = ROE / r`, and `P/S = Net Margin / r`. If you memorize these shortcuts, you can save 30 seconds of exam crunch-time.
*   **Payout Ratio Phrasing:** The curriculum uses `(1 - b)` to represent the Dividend Payout Ratio. If a vignette gives the Retention Rate ($b$) as 60%, do not blindly plug 0.60 into the numerator. The payout ratio is $1 - 0.60 = 0.40$ (or 40%).
*   **Mismatching $E_0$ and $E_1$:** If the exam asks for the *Trailing P/E*, but the table gives you the *Forward* P/E formula framework, remember that the only difference is the $(1 + g)$ term in the numerator. *Trailing* multiple numerators essentially compound the current metric forward one year.
