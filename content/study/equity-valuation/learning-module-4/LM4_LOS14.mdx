## Intuition Building

Price multiples (like P/E and P/B) strictly evaluate the value of the firm's *equity*. However, a company's total capital structure is a mix of both debt and equity. **Enterprise Value (EV)** measures the total cost to acquire the entire firm—meaning buying out all equity holders and assuming or paying off all debt. Because EV represents the total value of the firm, it must be compared to a metric generated by the entire firm before any capital providers are paid out. The premier metric for this is **EBITDA**. This LOS covers the calculation of EV, the rationales for using EV/EBITDA, and its structural limitations.

## Core Concept Teaching

**1. Calculating Enterprise Value (EV)**
Enterprise Value is the theoretical takeover price of a firm.
*   **Formula:** `EV = Market Value of Common Equity + Market Value of Preferred Equity + Market Value of Debt - Cash and Cash Equivalents`.
*   *Why subtract cash?* If you buy a company for $1 Billion, but the company has $200 Million in its bank account, you can immediately use that cash to pay yourself back or pay down the debt you just assumed. Your actual net cost to acquire the firm is only $800 Million.

**2. Rationales for Using EV/EBITDA**
Why do investment bankers and private equity analysts overwhelmingly prefer EV/EBITDA over the classic P/E ratio?
1.  **Capital Structure Neutrality:** P/E is heavily distorted by financial leverage. A company with massive debt will have massive interest expenses, crushing Net Income and artificially inflating the P/E ratio. EV/EBITDA evaluates the core operating earnings of the business *before* the impact of how the business chose to finance itself. It allows an apples-to-apples comparison between a debt-free company and a highly levered company.
2.  **Valuing Loss-Making Firms:** Depreciation and amortization (D&A) are massive non-cash expenses, especially for manufacturing and telecom firms. These expenses often drive Net Income negative, rendering the P/E multiple useless. EBITDA adds D&A back, frequently providing a positive denominator for valuation even when the firm is officially reporting net losses.
3.  **Cross-Border Comparisons:** Different countries have wildly different corporate tax rates and depreciation schedules. Because EBITDA is calculated before taxes and D&A, it normalizes these accounting and jurisdictional differences, making it the perfect tool for comparing a US firm to a French firm.

**3. Limitations of EV/EBITDA**
1.  **Ignoring Capital Intensity:** EBITDA completely ignores Capital Expenditures (CapEx). If two companies have identical EBITDA, but Company A requires $500M a year in equipment upgrades to survive while Company B requires $0, Company A is vastly inferior. EV/EBITDA is blind to this difference.
2.  **Ignoring Working Capital:** EBITDA overstates actual cash flow because it ignores the cash drained by necessary investments in inventory and accounts receivable.
3.  **Market Value of Debt Complexity:** Calculating EV requires the *market value* of debt. While the market value of equity (Market Cap) is effortlessly found on Yahoo Finance, corporate bonds are illiquid and bank loans are private. Analysts often must settle for the *book value* of debt, which degrades the accuracy of the EV calculation if interest rates have moved significantly since the debt was issued.

**Analyst Interpretation:**
When a private equity firm executes a Leveraged Buyout (LBO), they will completely rip out the target company's existing capital structure and replace it with their own massive debt load. Therefore, they do not care what the company's current interest expense or Net Income is. They only care about the core operational cash generation (EBITDA) that they can use to service *their* new debt. This is why EV/EBITDA is universally known as the "M&A Multiple."

**Exam Trap:**
The curriculum loves testing the "Cash Subtraction" rule in the EV formula. They will give you a vignette where a firm has 100 shares at $50, $2,000 in debt, and $500 in cash. A distractor answer will add the cash instead of subtracting it. Always remember: *Cash sitting in the target's bank account makes the acquisition cheaper for you.* Subtract it.

## Ground-Up Framework

**Step 1: Calculate Enterprise Value (EV)**
`EV = (Shares Outstanding × Current Share Price) + Market Value of Debt + Preferred Stock - (Cash + Cash Equivalents + Short-Term Investments)`

**Step 2: Calculate EBITDA**
`EBITDA = Net Income + Interest Expense + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization`
*(Or, working top-down: `EBITDA = Operating Income (EBIT) + D&A`)*

**Step 3: Calculate the Multiple**
**EV/EBITDA Multiple** = `EV / EBITDA`

## Examples / Applications

**Scenario: Evaluating a Capital Structure Arbitrage**
An analyst is comparing two competing logistics firms, Firm Alpha and Firm Beta.

**Firm Alpha (Levered):**
*   Stock Price: $25
*   Shares Outstanding: 40 Million
*   Market Value of Debt: $800 Million
*   Cash: $100 Million
*   Net Income: $50 Million
*   Interest + Taxes + D&A: $150 Million

**Firm Beta (Unlevered):**
*   Stock Price: $40
*   Shares Outstanding: 50 Million
*   Market Value of Debt: $0
*   Cash: $200 Million
*   Net Income: $100 Million
*   Interest + Taxes + D&A: $100 Million

*Step 1: Perform Market Checks (P/E Ratio)*
*   Alpha P/E: ($25 × 40M) / $50M = $1.0B / $50M = **20.0x P/E**
*   Beta P/E: ($40 × 50M) / $100M = $2.0B / $100M = **20.0x P/E**
*Analysis:* Based strictly on P/E, the market values both firms equally.

*Step 2: Calculate EV/EBITDA*
*   Alpha EV: $1.0B (Equity) + $800M (Debt) - $100M (Cash) = $1.7 Billion
*   Alpha EBITDA: $50M (NI) + $150M (Non-NI components) = $200 Million
*   **Alpha EV/EBITDA = 1.7B / 200M = 8.5x**

*   Beta EV: $2.0B (Equity) + $0 (Debt) - $200M (Cash) = $1.8 Billion
*   Beta EBITDA: $100M (NI) + $100M (Non-NI components) = $200 Million
*   **Beta EV/EBITDA = 1.8B / 200M = 9.0x**

*Direct Source Anchoring:* Matching the curriculum's emphasis on capital structure neutrality, the EV/EBITDA metric correctly reveals that Firm Alpha is actually cheaper operationally (8.5x vs 9.0x). The P/E multiples were identical only because Alpha's heavy interest expenses from its $800M debt load were artificially depressing its Net Income. EV/EBITDA strips away the financing noise to reveal the true operational valuation.

## Summary / Key Takeaways

*   **Book vs Market Value of Debt:** To calculate an accurate EV, you must use the *market value* of debt. However, if the prompt explicitly states "the market value of debt is unavailable," you are permitted to use the *book value* of debt as a proxy.
*   **The Mismatch Error:** The most legendary multiple trap is calculating `Enterprise Value / Net Income`. This is a fatal structural mismatch. Because EV includes debt, the denominator MUST be a metric calculated *before* interest payments are deducted (like EBITDA or EBIT). Dividing EV by an equity-only metric like Net Income will result in an immediate fail on that question.
*   **Preferred Stock Inclusion:** Do not forget that Preferred Stock is a senior claim similar to debt. Its market value must be added to the Market Cap alongside debt when computing Enterprise Value.
