## Intuition Building

When an analyst says a stock's actual P/E is 18, that is just a factual observation of the market price relative to earnings. But what *should* the P/E be? That is the essence of a **justified price multiple**. It is the multiple that the stock *deserves* based on its underlying fundamentals - its growth prospects, its risk profile, and its ability to generate cash payouts for shareholders.

If we can determine the justified P/E (the fair multiple) and compare it to the actual market P/E, we can quickly assess whether the stock is undervalued or overvalued. Misunderstanding this concept causes an analyst to blindly buy stocks with low P/Es, failing to realize that a low multiple might be entirely justified by terrible growth prospects or exceedingly high risk - a classic value trap.

## Ground-Up Framework

A justified P/E is most easily derived using the simplest Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model: the Gordon constant growth model.

<FormulaBlock label="Justified Forward P/E">
$$ \frac{P_0}{E_1} = \frac{D_1 / E_1}{r - g} = \frac{1 - b}{r - g} $$
</FormulaBlock>

<FormulaBlock label="Justified Trailing P/E">
$$ \frac{P_0}{E_0} = \frac{D_0(1 + g)/E_0}{r - g} = \frac{(1 - b)(1 + g)}{r - g} $$
</FormulaBlock>

Where:
- **$P_0$:** Current intrinsic value of the stock
- **$E_1$ / $E_0$:** Next year's / Current year's earnings
- **$D_1$ / $D_0$:** Next year's / Current year's dividend
- **$r$:** Required rate of return (risk)
- **$g$:** Expected constant growth rate of dividends
- **$b$:** Retention rate (the portion of earnings kept by the company, $1 - \text{payout ratio}$)
- **$1 - b$:** Dividend payout ratio

## Core Concept Teaching

The justified price multiple bridges absolute valuation (the intrinsic DCF value) and relative valuation (price multiples).

<Callout type="key-concept">
**The Fundamental Drivers of P/E**
Reviewing the structure of the Gordon Growth equation, we clearly see that, all else being equal, a justified P/E is:
1. **Positively related to growth ($g$):** Higher expected cash flow or dividend growth justifies a higher multiple.
2. **Inversely related to risk ($r$):** A higher required rate of return drives the intrinsic value down, which justifies a lower multiple.
3. **Positively related to the dividend payout ratio ($1 - b$):** Assuming $r > g$, a higher payout ratio increases the justified multiple for a stable growth firm.
</Callout>

**Deep Analyst Interpretation:**
For a practicing analyst, this math clarifies market reactions. If the central bank suddenly hikes interest rates, $r$ increases across the market. The P/E multiple model tells us instantly that *all justified P/Es must contract*. Knowing this prevents a portfolio manager from assuming that all stocks have suddenly become cheap. They simply possess lower justified multiples because macro risk is now higher.

<Callout type="trap">
**Exam Trap: The Value Trap Illusion**
A stock trading at an actual P/E of 8x might look incredibly cheap compared to the sector average of 15x. However, if its cost of equity ($r$) is sky-high and its growth ($g$) is negative, its fundamental justified P/E might calculate out to only 6x. The stock is not cheap; it is actually a value trap and overvalued at 8x.
</Callout>

### Complex DCF Derivation

The Gordon Growth Model gives us a clean, closed-form equation tailored for mature, dividend-paying companies. But we do not have to use it for complex multi-stage companies. If an analyst uses a complex, multi-stage Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) model, they simply take the final intrinsic value ($P_0$) output by that model and divide it by the forecasted earnings ($E_1$ or $E_0$) to express the absolute valuation in the familiar form of a relative multiple:

$$ \text{Justified P/E} = \frac{\text{DCF Intrinsic Value}}{\text{Estimated EPS}} $$

<Callout type="trap">
**Exam Trap: Trailing vs. Forward Conflation**
When you use a DCF intrinsic value to derive a P/E, be exceptionally careful which EPS you divide by. Dividing $P_0$ by $E_1$ gives the justified *forward* P/E. Dividing $P_0$ by $E_0$ gives the justified *trailing* P/E. The exam will often try to trick you by offering both and asking for the specific one.
</Callout>

## Examples / Applications

<WorkedExample title="Forward P/E Based on Fundamental Forecasts (Gordon Growth Model)">
**Source Anchor:** Drawn structurally from the CFA curriculum methodology for BP p.l.c.

**Scenario:**
An energy analyst forecasts a long-term earnings retention rate ($b$) for an oil company of 40% and a long-term constant earnings growth rate ($g$) of 3.5%. Given significant legal uncertainties, the analyst sets a high required rate of return ($r$) of 7.6%.

**Calculate the justified forward P/E.**

**Solution:**
First, identify the dividend payout ratio ($1 - b$): 1 - 0.40 = 0.60, or 60%.

Using the justified forward P/E formula:
$$ \frac{P_0}{E_1} = \frac{1 - b}{r - g} $$
$$ \frac{P_0}{E_1} = \frac{0.60}{0.076 - 0.035} = \frac{0.60}{0.041} = 14.63 $$

**Deep Analyst Interpretation:**
The fundamental characteristics of this company dictate that a fair price for its cash flow generation would be 14.6x forward earnings. If the stock is currently trading in the open market at a forward P/E of 10x, it is considered undervalued. If it is trading at 20x, it is overvalued.
</WorkedExample>

<WorkedExample title="Forward P/E Based on Fundamental Forecasts (Complex DCF)">
**Source Anchor:** Directly derived from the CFA curriculum Toyota FCFE example.

**Scenario:**
You use a multi-stage Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) model to value a massive vehicle manufacturer. Your complex DCF outputs an intrinsic value of JPY 6,980 for the stock. Your forecasted next-year EPS for the company is JPY 720. The current market price of the stock is JPY 6,688.

1. What is the stock's justified forward P/E?
2. Is the stock overvalued or undervalued?

**Solution:**
1. $$ \text{Justified Forward P/E} = \frac{\text{DCF Value}}{\text{Estimated EPS}} = \frac{6,980}{720} = 9.69x $$
2. The stock's actual forward P/E based on the market price is $6,688 / 720 = 9.29x$. Because the justified multiple (9.69x) is higher than the actual multiple (9.29x), the stock is currently undervalued by the market.
</WorkedExample>

## Summary / Key Takeaways

- **The Definition:** A justified price multiple is the fair value of a multiple, calculated using the company's internal fundamental cash flow drivers instead of external market comparables.
- **The Drivers:** The justified P/E expands if the expected growth rate ($g$) rises or if the payout ratio ($1 - b$) rises. It contracts if the required return ($r$) rises.
- **Two Ways to Calculate:** You can either use the closed-form Gordon Growth Model formulas for trailing and forward P/E, or you can run a complex multi-stage DCF and divide the resulting intrinsic value by estimated EPS.
- **The Golden Rule:** If justified multiple > actual market multiple, the stock is undervalued. If justified multiple < actual market multiple, the stock is overvalued.
