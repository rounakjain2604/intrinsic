## STAGE 2: TEACHING

### ① THE WHY: BUILD THE INTUITION FIRST

**Why does this LOS exist?**

Before you can analyze a bank or insurance company, you must understand *why the rules of analysis are different*. If you apply the same analytical framework to J.P. Morgan that you apply to Apple or Toyota, you will make catastrophic errors.

**The fundamental difference comes down to one idea: interconnectedness.**

A steel company fails → its suppliers and customers are hurt. The economy adjusts.

A major bank fails → *every entity that has deposits at that bank, every counterparty to its trades, every borrower depending on its credit lines, every other bank that lent to it* — all are simultaneously impaired. The shock propagates outward in a chain reaction. This is **systemic risk**.

This is not hypothetical. Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy in September 2008 nearly collapsed the entire global financial system. The government had to step in with trillions of dollars because the alternative — letting the chain reaction continue — was economic collapse.

**Why this matters to an analyst:**
- You cannot evaluate a bank without evaluating the regulatory environment constraining it
- You cannot evaluate a bank's assets the same way you evaluate a manufacturer's assets
- The very composition and structure of a bank's balance sheet — mostly financial assets funded by deposits — creates risk exposures that don't exist for non-financial companies

---

### ② THE CONCEPT: TEACH IT FROM THE GROUND UP

#### 2.1 The Systemic Importance of Banks

Banks serve as **intermediaries** — they sit between capital providers (depositors) and capital users (borrowers). This creates a web of financial inter-linkages across:
- Households
- Other banks
- Corporations
- Governments

> **Key insight:** The failure of one bank sends shockwaves through this web. The *larger* the bank and the more *widespread* its linkages, the greater the damage. This is why the concept of "too big to fail" exists — not because governments want to reward reckless behavior, but because the cost of letting a major bank fail exceeds the cost of rescuing it.

**Bank runs:** Even the *expectation* that a bank might not honor its deposits causes depositors to withdraw — a self-fulfilling prophecy. This is why most countries have **deposit insurance** (up to stated limits).

#### 2.2 Financial Assets vs. Tangible Assets

This is a critical distinction for analysts:

**Non-financial company (e.g., manufacturer):**
- Assets: Factories, equipment, inventory, land
- These assets produce goods
- Value is based on productive capacity, depreciation, physical condition
- Most are carried at historical cost

**Financial company (e.g., bank):**
- Assets: **Loans, securities, derivatives, interbank deposits**
- These assets *are* the product — the bank's "factory" is its loan book
- Value depends on counterparty creditworthiness, interest rates, market conditions
- Many are measured at **fair value**, creating direct P&L and balance sheet volatility

> **Analyst takeaway:** When you analyze a bank's assets, you are really analyzing the credit quality of its borrowers, the market risk of its securities portfolio, and the interest rate sensitivity of its entire balance sheet. The concept of "asset quality" is fundamentally about credit risk — not physical condition.

#### 2.3 Deposit-Dominated Liabilities

For most banks, **deposits constitute >80% of total liabilities** (per the curriculum, citing US commercial banks as of December 2016).

This creates a unique vulnerability:
- Deposits are demand obligations — the bank must pay them on request
- But the bank has lent the money out in long-term loans
- This **maturity mismatch** (short-term funding, long-term assets) is the fundamental source of bank liquidity risk
- If depositors demand their money back faster than loans are repaid → liquidity crisis

**Why deposits are insured:** Because a bank's failure to honor deposits could trigger contagion across the economy. Deposit insurance removes depositors' incentive to panic.

#### 2.4 Types of Financial Institutions

The curriculum provides an extensive taxonomy in **Exhibit 1**. For exam purposes, know:

| Category | Examples | Key Features |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Basic Banking** | Commercial banks, credit unions, building societies | Deposit-taking, loan-making; credit unions are member-owned nonprofits |
| **Investment Industry Intermediaries** | Mutual funds, hedge funds, brokers/dealers | Pool and manage money; vary in transparency and regulation |
| **Insurers** | P&C insurers, L&H insurers, reinsurers | Protect against adverse events; reinsurers sell insurance to other insurers |
| **Specialized** | Mortgage banks, trust banks, online payment companies | Focused on specific financial services |

> **Exam note:** The curriculum explicitly states that **this reading focuses primarily on banks and insurance companies** — these are the two you need to analyze in depth.

#### 2.5 Global Systemic Risk: Banks vs. Insurance

**Important distinction:**
- **Banks** have high systemic risk because of extensive cross-border inter-linkages (deposits, lending, counterparty relationships)
- **Insurance companies** have lower systemic risk because:
  - Smaller proportion of cross-border business (except reinsurance)
  - Foreign branches must hold assets adequate to cover liabilities in each jurisdiction
  - **Reinsurance** is the exception — it is largely international and can serve as a transmission mechanism for global shocks

## STAGE 2: TEACHING

### ① THE WHY: BUILD THE INTUITION FIRST

Banks borrow short and lend long. This fundamental mismatch makes them inherently fragile. Left entirely to market forces:
- Banks would hold minimal capital (to maximize ROE)
- Banks would take excessive risks (to earn higher yields)
- During crises, the system would collapse because interconnected banks would fail simultaneously

**Regulation exists to prevent this.** The Basel framework forces banks to hold a minimum cushion of capital that can absorb losses before depositors are harmed. It also forces banks to maintain adequate liquidity so they can survive short-term stress events.

> **Think of it this way:** A bank's capital is its airbag. Basel III tells you how thick the airbag must be and whether it's made of the right material.

---

### ② THE CONCEPT: TEACH IT FROM THE GROUND UP

#### 2.1 The Basel Committee

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision is a standing committee of the **Bank for International Settlements (BIS)**. It includes representatives from central banks and bank regulatory agencies from around the world. It sets **international standards** that individual jurisdictions then implement into their own laws.

**Basel III** (the current framework) was developed in response to the 2008 financial crisis and contains:
1. **Minimum capital requirements** (Pillar 1)
2. **Supervisory review process** (Pillar 2) — regulators review banks' internal risk management
3. **Market discipline** (Pillar 3) — disclosure requirements for transparency

#### 2.2 Capital Requirements — The Heart of Basel III

The central idea: **A bank must fund a minimum percentage of its risk-weighted assets with its own capital.** This prevents excessive leverage.

#### 2.2.1 Types of Regulatory Capital

Basel III creates a **capital hierarchy** based on loss-absorbing quality:

| Capital Tier | Components | Loss Absorption Quality |
|-------------|------------|------------------------|
| **CET1 (Common Equity Tier 1)** | Common shares, retained earnings, AOCI, minority interest (less regulatory adjustments like goodwill) | **Highest** — absorbs losses first while bank is a going concern |
| **Additional Tier 1 (AT1)** | Non-cumulative perpetual preferred equity, hybrid instruments | **High** — absorbs losses while bank is a going concern |
| **Tier 2** | Subordinated debt ≥5yr original maturity, certain reserves | **Lower** — absorbs losses only upon liquidation |

> **Critical detail from curriculum:** CET1 is reduced by **goodwill and other intangibles**. This is because these assets have uncertain value in a distress scenario — they cannot be converted to cash to pay depositors. *This is a high-priority exam point.*

**Why the hierarchy matters:**
- CET1 is the *purest* form of capital — it is always available to absorb losses
- AT1 can absorb losses while the bank is still operating (going concern)
- Tier 2 only absorbs losses in bankruptcy (gone concern)
- Regulators care most about CET1 because it is the most reliable buffer

#### 2.2.2 Risk-Weighted Assets (RWAs)

Not all assets carry the same risk. Basel III assigns **risk weights** to each asset category:

| Asset | Risk Weight | RWA Contribution |
|-------|------------|-----------------|
| Cash, government securities | 0% | $0 |
| Interbank claims | 20% | 20% of face value |
| Residential mortgages | 50% | 50% of face value |
| Corporate loans | 100% | 100% of face value |

**Example:** A bank has $100M in government bonds and $200M in corporate loans.
- RWA from bonds: $100M × 0% = $0
- RWA from corporate loans: $200M × 100% = $200M
- **Total RWA = $200M**

#### 2.2.3 Minimum Capital Ratios

| Ratio | Formula | Minimum |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **CET1 Capital Ratio** | CET1 / Total RWA | **4.5%** |
| **Tier 1 Capital Ratio** | (CET1 + AT1) / Total RWA | **6.0%** |
| **Total Capital Ratio** | (CET1 + AT1 + Tier 2) / Total RWA | **8.0%** |

> **Exam requirement: You must be able to calculate these ratios from balance sheet data.** See the practice questions below.

#### 2.2.4 The Leverage Ratio

The risk-based capital ratios have a weakness: if risk weights are too low (or manipulated), a bank could still be dangerously leveraged. The **leverage ratio** addresses this by ignoring risk weights entirely:

$$\text{Leverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Tier 1 Capital}}{\text{Total Assets (unweighted)}} \geq 3\%$$

> **Key distinction:** The leverage ratio uses *Tier 1 capital* (not CET1 alone) and *unweighted total assets* (not risk-weighted). It acts as a **backstop** to the risk-based ratios.

#### 2.2.5 Capital Buffers

Basel III requires banks to hold capital **above** the minimums in good times, so it can be drawn down during stress:

| Buffer | Size | Purpose |
|--------|------|---------|
| **Capital Conservation Buffer** | 2.5% of RWA (in CET1) | Absorb losses during periods of financial or economic stress; restrictions on dividends/buybacks if breached |
| **Countercyclical Buffer** | 0–2.5% of RWA (in CET1) | National regulators impose this during credit booms to create additional cushion |

**Effective minimums (including conservation buffer):**
- CET1: 4.5% + 2.5% = **7.0%**
- Tier 1: 6.0% + 2.5% = **8.5%**
- Total: 8.0% + 2.5% = **10.5%**

### Blue Box Example 1: Impact on CET1 Capital

**From the curriculum (Exhibit 3):**

A bank has the following:
- Common shares: $22 billion
- Retained earnings: $7 billion
- AOCI: –$1 billion
- Minority interest: $1 billion
- **Total CET1 before adjustments: $29 billion**
- Less: Goodwill: –$6 billion
- Less: Other intangibles: –$2 billion
- **CET1 Capital: $21 billion**
- Total RWA: $300 billion
- **CET1 Ratio: 21/300 = 7.0%** (≥ 4.5% minimum ✓)

**Key teaching point:** Goodwill and intangibles are **subtracted** because they cannot be converted to cash in a crisis. Without this deduction, the bank would report $29B in CET1, vastly overstating its true loss-absorbing capacity.

---

### Blue Box Example 2: CET1 Sensitivity

**From the curriculum (Exhibit 5):**

The exercise asks: If the bank experiences a $5 billion loss from an economic downturn, what happens to its CET1 ratio?

- CET1 falls from $21 billion to $16 billion
- CET1 Ratio = 16/300 = **5.33%**
- Still above 4.5% minimum, but now **below the 7.0% effective minimum** (including capital conservation buffer)
- This triggers restrictions on the bank's ability to pay dividends and make share buybacks

> **Analyst takeaway:** Capital ratios look healthy in good times. The true test is whether they survive stress scenarios. This is why stress testing is mandatory under Basel III.

### 2.3 Liquidity Requirements

#### 2.3.1 Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

$$\text{LCR} = \frac{\text{High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA)}}{\text{Expected Net Cash Outflows over 30 days}} \geq 100\%$$

**Purpose:** Ensure the bank can survive a **30-day stress scenario** where depositors and creditors are withdrawing funds.

- **HQLA** = cash, central bank reserves, high-quality government bonds (immediately convertible to cash)
- **Net cash outflows** = outflows minus limited inflows over 30 days under prescribed stress assumptions

> **Intuition:** If LCR = 121%, the bank can survive cash outflows that are 21% higher than its stress-scenario estimate, or equivalently, it can withstand the stress scenario for 36.3 days (121% × 30 days).

#### 2.3.2 Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

$$\text{NSFR} = \frac{\text{Available Stable Funding}}{\text{Required Stable Funding}} \geq 100\%$$

**Purpose:** Ensure the bank has adequate **longer-term** stable funding sources relative to the liquidity profile of its assets.

| Available Stable Funding (numerator) | Required Stable Funding (denominator) |
|-------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Total deposits, long-term debt, equity | Total investments, total loans, goodwill, intangibles, other assets |

> **Key distinction:** LCR = **short-term** liquidity strength (can you survive 30 days?). NSFR = **structural** funding strength (are your long-term assets funded by stable, long-term sources?).

## STAGE 2: TEACHING

### ① THE WHY

CAMELS was originally developed by US bank regulators as an internal supervisory rating system. Analysts have adopted it because it provides a **structured, comprehensive framework** that systematically covers all the risk dimensions unique to banking.

Without a framework like CAMELS, an analyst might focus on profitability while ignoring liquidity, or analyze capital without considering asset quality. CAMELS forces discipline.

### ② EACH COMPONENT IN DEPTH

---

#### C — CAPITAL ADEQUACY

**What it measures:** Whether the bank has enough capital to absorb losses without threatening its viability or requiring external support.

**Key ratios:**

| Ratio | Formula | Basel III Minimum |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| CET1 Capital Ratio | CET1 / RWA | 4.5% (7.0% with buffer) |
| Tier 1 Capital Ratio | Tier 1 / RWA | 6.0% (8.5% with buffer) |
| Total Capital Ratio | Total Capital / RWA | 8.0% (10.5% with buffer) |
| Leverage Ratio | Tier 1 / Total Assets | 3.0% |

**What the analyst should evaluate:**
1. **Level of capital ratios relative to minimums** — are they comfortably above or barely meeting requirements?
2. **Trend** — are ratios improving or declining over time?
3. **Composition** — is capital shifting from CET1 (highest quality) toward Tier 2 (lower quality)? This is a *deterioration* even if the total capital ratio is stable
4. **Capital vs. RWA growth** — if RWA grows faster than capital, ratios decline

**Case Study — Citigroup (Exhibit 6):**

| | 2016 | 2015 | 2014 |
|-|------|------|------|
| CET1 Ratio | 12.55% | 12.07% | 11.65% |
| Tier 1 Ratio | 14.29% | 13.56% | 12.95% |
| Total Capital Ratio | 17.33% | 16.52% | 15.92% |
| Leverage Ratio | 8.10% | 7.83% | 7.35% |

**Observations:**
- All ratios improved consistently from 2014 to 2016
- All are well above Basel III minimums
- Rating: **1** (strongest) — justified by consistent improvement and comfortable margins above minimums

---

#### A — ASSET QUALITY

**What it measures:** The credit quality of the bank's loan portfolio and other assets, and the soundness of its risk management.

**This is one of the most critical components** because impaired loans directly reduce capital and can threaten solvency.

**Key metrics and analysis:**

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|--------|------------------|
| **Composition of assets** | What % of assets are loans vs securities vs trading assets? Higher loan concentration = higher credit risk |
| **Loan mix (consumer vs commercial)** | Different risk profiles; commercial can be more concentrated in individual borrowers |
| **Credit quality profile** | Breakdown by quality tier (strong, good, satisfactory, substandard, past due, impaired) |
| **Allowance for loan losses vs. actual charge-offs** | Is the bank provisioning enough? Allowance/net charge-offs ratio is key |
| **Provision for loan losses vs. net charge-offs** | If provision < net charge-offs, the bank is drawing down its allowance — a warning sign |
| **Impairment trend** | Declining impairments = improving asset quality; increasing = deterioration |

**Loan Loss Analysis — Three Key Ratios:**

1. **Allowance for Loan Losses / Net Charge-Offs** — measures the cushion. A declining ratio means the bank is eating into its reserves.

2. **Provision for Loan Losses / Net Charge-Offs** — measures whether current provisioning covers current losses. 
   - Ratio > 1: provision exceeds losses → building reserves (conservative)
   - Ratio < 1: provision is less than losses → drawing down reserves (aggressive)

3. **Net Charge-Off Ratio** — net charge-offs / average loans. Rising ratio = deteriorating credit quality.

**Case Study — Citigroup Asset Quality (Exhibits 7–13):**

Key findings from Citigroup:
- Loans represent 41.4% of total assets (2016) — the single largest asset category
- Consumer loans > commercial loans
- Allowance for consumer loan losses / net charge-offs: 2.31× (2016) vs 2.58× (2014) — **declining cushion**
- Provision for consumer loan losses / net charge-offs: 1.01× (2016) — barely keeping up
- Commercial loans improving: allowance / net charge-offs 5.84× (2016) vs 3.45× (2014)
- Overall rating assigned: **2.5** — above average for commercial loans, below average for consumer loans

---

#### M — MANAGEMENT CAPABILITIES

**What it measures:** Management's ability to identify profitable opportunities while appropriately managing associated risks.

**This is the most qualitative and subjective component.** There are no standard ratios.

**How to evaluate:**
- Track record of strategic decisions (acquisitions, dispositions, capital allocation)
- Revenue growth vs. risk growth — is the bank growing earnings by taking excessive risks?
- Risk management practices — quality of loan underwriting, hedging effectiveness
- Compensation structure — does it promote long-term value or excessive risk-taking?
- Regulatory actions — any enforcement actions, consent orders, or fines?

**Case Study — Citigroup Management (Exhibits 14–17):**

Key findings:
- Revenue mix showed declining net interest income offset by trading gains → **trading income is less sustainable**
- Provision for credit losses decreased significantly, boosting earnings → **unsustainable earnings driver**
- 84% of pretax earnings increases (2012–2016) came from provision decreases, not revenue growth
- Rating: **2** — competent but with concerns about sustainability of earnings growth

---

#### E — EARNINGS SUFFICIENCY

**What it measures:** Whether the bank's returns are adequate relative to its cost of capital, and whether earnings quality is high.

**Key analysis dimensions:**

**1. Net Interest Margin (NIM)**

$$\text{NIM} = \frac{\text{Net Interest Income}}{\text{Average Interest-Earning Assets}}$$

This is the **core profitability metric** for banks. It measures the spread between what the bank earns on loans/investments and what it pays on deposits/borrowings.

**NIM analysis uses average balance tables:** These show average asset/liability volumes, interest earned/paid, and average rates. They reveal exactly *where* the NIM is coming from and how it's changing.

**2. Revenue Mix and Quality**
- **Net interest income** = most sustainable → highest quality
- **Fee income** (service charges, trust fees) = relatively stable → moderate quality
- **Trading income** = volatile, dependent on market conditions → lowest quality
- **Provision for loan losses decreases** = can boost earnings but are one-time → very low quality

**3. Key Profitability Ratios**
- Return on equity (ROE) and return on assets (ROA)
- Pre-provision operating profit as % of assets
- Cost-to-income ratio (efficiency ratio)

**Case Study — Citigroup Earnings (Exhibits 18–21):**

Key findings:
- NIM declined from 2.90% (2014) to 2.86% (2016) — small but notable decline
- US offices NIM improved (2.88% → 3.25%), but non-US offices declined significantly (2.91% → 2.40%)
- Non-US NIM decline attributed to strengthening dollar and lower international rates
- Trading income rose while net interest income fell → quality concerns
- Rating: **3** — mediocre earnings quality driven by unsustainable factors

> **Critical analytical insight:** Analyzing average balances and rates is more useful for *explaining the past* than *projecting the future*. The curriculum notes that **end-of-period balances and rates make a better starting point for projections.**

---

#### L — LIQUIDITY POSITION

**What it measures:** Whether the bank has enough liquid assets to meet its short-term and medium-term obligations.

**Key ratios (from Basel III):**

| Ratio | Formula | Minimum | Time Horizon |
|-------|---------|---------|-------------|
| LCR | HQLA / Expected Net Cash Outflows (30 days) | 100% | Short-term (30 days) |
| NSFR | Available Stable Funding / Required Stable Funding | 100% | Medium-term (structural) |

**Case Study — Citigroup Liquidity (Exhibits 22–23):**

| | Dec 2016 | Sep 2016 | Dec 2015 |
|-|---------|---------|---------|
| LCR | 121% | 120% | 113% |
| NSFR (approximate) | 120% | 121% | 118% |

Key observations:
- Both ratios above 100% and improving
- LCR of 121% → can withstand stress for 36.3 days (121% × 30) or outflows 21% higher than baseline
- Rating: **1** — strong liquidity position

---

#### S — SENSITIVITY TO MARKET RISK

**What it measures:** How changes in interest rates, exchange rates, equity prices, and commodity prices affect the bank's earnings and balance sheet.

**Key tool: Value at Risk (VaR)**

VaR answers: "What is the worst expected loss over a single day at a 99% confidence level?"

**Important VaR limitations:**
- VaR is a **single-day** measure — prolonged market dislocations can cause cumulative losses far exceeding VaR
- VaR calculation assumptions differ across banks → **useful for intra-company trend analysis, NOT for cross-company comparison**
- The 1% tail (events beyond the 99% confidence level) can involve catastrophic losses not captured by VaR

**Additional sensitivity metrics:**
- **Trading revenue / Average daily VaR** — measures reward-to-risk for trading operations
- **Capital ratio sensitivity tables** — show the impact of incremental changes in capital or RWA on capital ratios

**Case Study — Citigroup Sensitivity (Exhibits 24–25):**

Key observations:
- Average trading VaR declined from $92M (2015) to $70M (2016)
- Total VaR is <1% of net income and ~0.1% of equity → manageable market risk
- Capital ratio sensitivity to $100M capital change or $1B RWA change is negligible (<1 bps)
- Rating: **1** — well-controlled market risk

### ③ OVERALL CAMELS SCORE — HOW IT WORKS

After rating each component (1 = strongest, 5 = weakest), the composite score can be calculated:

**Unweighted approach:** Sum all 6 ratings, divide by 6

**Weighted approach:** An analyst may weight components based on priority. For example, an equity analyst might place 2× weight on Asset Quality and Earnings.

**Citigroup Example (Exhibit 26):**

| Component | Rating | Weight | Weighted Rating |
|-----------|--------|--------|----------------|
| Capital | 1.0 | 1 | 1.00 |
| Asset Quality | 2.5 | 2 | 5.00 |
| Management | 2.0 | 1 | 2.00 |
| Earnings | 3.0 | 2 | 6.00 |
| Liquidity | 1.0 | 1 | 1.00 |
| Sensitivity | 1.0 | 1 | 1.00 |
| **Total** | **10.5** | **8** | **16.00** |
| **CAMELS Rating** | **1.75** | | **2.00** |

> Notice: The **unweighted** rating (10.5/6 = 1.75) vs **weighted** (16/8 = 2.00) — weighting matters! By double-weighting the components with higher (worse) ratings, the overall assessment looks weaker.

### ④ LIMITATIONS OF CAMELS

**This is explicitly tested.** From the curriculum and end-of-chapter questions:

CAMELS covers the six main risk dimensions but **does NOT address**:
1. **Competitive environment** — the bank's position relative to peers
2. **Government support** — whether the bank has implicit or explicit government backing
3. **Mission and corporate culture** — some banks are more aggressive than others
4. **Off-balance-sheet items** — contingent liabilities, operating leases, securitized assets
5. **Segment information** — performance variation across business units or geographies
6. **Currency exposure** — particularly for internationally active banks
7. **Risk disclosures** — quality and transparency of risk-related disclosures

> **Exam focus from EOC Q3:** The correct answer to "What is a limitation of CAMELS?" is the competitive environment. CAMELS addresses sensitivity to market risk (so that's NOT a limitation) and management capabilities (also NOT a limitation).

### ⑤ BRIEF OVERVIEW: DERIVATIVES ACCOUNTING

The curriculum includes a brief but testable overview:

| Concept | Accounting Treatment |
|---------|---------------------|
| At inception | Many derivatives have zero value → only the **notional amount** is disclosed in notes |
| Post-inception | Mark-to-market creates an asset or liability |
| **Cash flow hedge or net investment hedge** | Changes in value → **OCI** (recognized in income over life of hedged transaction) |
| **Fair value hedge or freestanding derivative** | Changes in value → **income statement** immediately |
| **Failed hedge classification** | Immediate P&L recognition → can cause earnings volatility and may require additional collateral |

> **Exam relevance:** If a derivative is freestanding (not classified as a hedge), its fair value changes flow through the income statement, creating **earnings volatility**. This is directly tested in EOC Q18.

## STAGE 2: TEACHING — THE ANALYTICAL TOOLKIT

### ① CAPITAL ADEQUACY ANALYSIS: STEP-BY-STEP

**Step 1: Gather capital ratios from the bank's regulatory disclosures**

| Ratio | Source in financial statements |
|-------|-------------------------------|
| CET1 Ratio | Regulatory filing or notes to financial statements |
| Tier 1 Ratio | Same |
| Total Capital Ratio | Same |
| Leverage Ratio | Same |

**Step 2: Compare to minimums and assess trend**

| Assessment | What you're looking for |
|------------|------------------------|
| **Absolute level** | All ratios well above Basel III minimums (including conservation buffers)? |
| **Trend** | Improving, stable, or deteriorating? |
| **Composition shift** | Is the bank replacing CET1 with lower-quality Tier 2 capital? If yes → deterioration even if total ratio is stable |
| **RWA growth** | Growing faster than capital? → Declining ratios |

**Step 3: Calculate if data is given in raw form**

From the curriculum's Citigroup example (and EOC Q9), you must be able to calculate:

$$\text{CET1 Ratio} = \frac{\text{CET1 Capital}}{\text{Total RWA}}$$

$$\text{Tier 1 Ratio} = \frac{\text{CET1} + \text{AT1}}{\text{Total RWA}}$$

$$\text{Total Capital Ratio} = \frac{\text{CET1} + \text{AT1} + \text{Tier 2}}{\text{Total RWA}}$$

> **Exam trap from EOC Q9:** CET1 and Tier 1 ratios can improve while the Total Capital Ratio declines. This signals "mixed" conditions — the bank is improving its core capital quality while total capital (including Tier 2) is declining. The correct answer is **mixed**, not simply improving or declining.

---

### ② ASSET QUALITY ANALYSIS: STEP-BY-STEP

**Step 1: Assess asset composition**

From the balance sheet, compute:
- Loans as % of total assets
- Consumer loans vs commercial loans breakdown
- Investments as % of total assets
- Liquid assets as % of total assets

**What declining liquid assets % means:** The bank is becoming less liquid. If total loans as % of assets is growing while liquid assets shrink → increased risk profile. (Tested in EOC Q10.)

**Step 2: Analyze credit quality profile**

Banks disclose loan quality in categories (strong, good, satisfactory, substandard, past due, impaired). Track:
- Is the proportion of strong credit quality loans increasing?
- Is the proportion of impaired/past-due loans decreasing?
- Are impairment allowances keeping pace with changes?

> **Key insight from EOC Q11:** Impairment allowances should track the risk indicator they're designed to cover. The curriculum shows that YoY changes in impairment allowances most closely tracked YoY changes in **past due but not impaired** assets — the bank was provisioning in *anticipation* of these loans becoming impaired.

**Step 3: Loan loss analysis (the money question)**

Calculate and interpret these ratios:

| Ratio | Formula | Interpretation |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Allowance / Net Charge-Offs** | Allowance for Loan Losses ÷ Net Charge-Offs | Cushion ratio. Declining = bank is eating into reserves. |
| **Provision / Net Charge-Offs** | Provision for Loan Losses ÷ Net Charge-Offs | Current coverage. <1 means provisioning lags actual losses. >1 means provisioning exceeds current losses (building reserves). |

**Worked Example from EOC Q12:**

| Year | Consumer ALL / NCO | Commercial ALL / NCO |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 2015 | 13,000/2,901 = **4.48×** | 169/44 = **3.84×** |
| 2016 | 11,500/2,505 = **4.59×** | 1,012/387 = **2.61×** |
| 2017 | 11,000/2,460 = **4.47×** | 1,540/1,060 = **1.45×** |

**Consumer cushion:** Relatively stable (4.47–4.59×). Not a major concern.

**Commercial cushion:** Plummeting from 3.84× to 1.45×. The bank's allowance for commercial loan losses is barely exceeding its actual commercial charge-offs. This is the **correct answer** — the cushion has declined.

Also check provision coverage:

| Year | Consumer PLL / NCO | Commercial PLL / NCO |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 2015 | 1,300/2,901 = **0.45×** | 95/44 = **2.16×** |
| 2016 | 2,000/2,505 = **0.80×** | 442/387 = **1.14×** |
| 2017 | 3,000/2,460 = **1.22×** | 1,100/1,060 = **1.04×** |

**Consumer provisioning:** Improving from 0.45× to 1.22× — bank has become more conservative (not less). Eliminates wrong answer "less conservative consumer provisioning."

**Commercial provisioning:** Declining from 2.16× to 1.04× — barely covering actual losses. While commercial provisions exceed charge-offs (ratio > 1), the margin is rapidly shrinking.

### ③ EARNINGS ANALYSIS: STEP-BY-STEP

**Step 1: Revenue quality assessment**

Decompose total revenue into:
1. **Net interest income** (NII) — highest quality, most sustainable
2. **Fee and commission income** — relatively stable
3. **Trading income** — volatile, opportunistic → lowest quality
4. **Gains from provision for loan loss decreases** — unsustainable boost

> **Key principle:** If a growing share of revenue comes from trading or provision decreases, earnings quality is declining — even if total earnings are growing.

**Step 2: Net Interest Margin analysis using average balance tables**

Banks publish average balance tables showing, for each asset/liability category:
- Average volume
- Interest earned/paid
- Average rate

**How to use this:**
1. Calculate total NIM = Net Interest Revenue / Average Interest-Earning Assets
2. Decompose by geography (US vs non-US) to find where margins are improving/declining
3. Link changes in NIM to specific causes (FX translation, rate environment, portfolio changes)

**Citigroup finding:** US NIM improved from 2.88% (2014) to 3.25% (2016), but non-US NIM declined from 2.91% (2014) to 2.40% (2016). Overall NIM declined slightly (2.90% to 2.86%).

> **Analyst insight from curriculum:** "End-of-period balances and their associated interest rates may make a better starting point for projecting future earnings than the average balance information." Average balances explain the past; they don't predict the future.

**Step 3: Provision impact on earnings**

Calculate: What percentage of pretax earnings growth is attributable to provision decreases?

Citigroup: **84% of pretax earnings increases** (2012–2016) came from reduced provisions, not from revenue growth. This is a major quality concern.

---

### ④ LIQUIDITY ANALYSIS: STEP-BY-STEP

**Step 1: Calculate LCR**

$$\text{LCR} = \frac{\text{HQLA}}{\text{Net Cash Outflows}} \geq 100\%$$

**Interpreting LCR:**
- LCR = 121% → Can survive 30 × 1.21 = **36.3 days** of stress, OR can handle outflows 21% higher than the stress estimate
- LCR trending upward = strengthening liquidity

**Step 2: Calculate NSFR**

$$\text{NSFR} = \frac{\text{Available Stable Funding (deposits + LT debt + equity)}}{\text{Required Stable Funding (investments + loans + goodwill + intangibles + other)}} \geq 100\%$$

**Interpreting NSFR:**
- Measures whether the bank has stable, long-term funding sources to cover its less-liquid, longer-term assets
- NSFR > 100% = structural funding is adequate

---

### ⑤ SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS: STEP-BY-STEP

**Step 1: Assess VaR levels and trends**

- Declining VaR → reduced market risk exposure (improving)
- Compare VaR to net income and equity → should be negligible (<1% of net income is typical for well-managed banks)

**Step 2: Assess VaR with nuance**

From EOC Q7: Declining VaR over several years **= improvement** in sensitivity to market risk.

**Step 3: Additional sensitivity measures**
- Trading revenue / Average daily VaR = reward-to-risk ratio
- Capital ratio sensitivity tables = impact of marginal capital/RWA changes

## STAGE 2: TEACHING

### ① THE SEVEN NON-CAMELS FACTORS

The curriculum identifies the following factors that deserve analyst attention beyond the CAMELS framework:

---

#### 1. GOVERNMENT SUPPORT

**Why it matters:** Some banks have explicit or implicit government backing. This fundamentally changes the bank's risk profile and credit analysis.

- **Explicit support:** Government-owned banks or banks with formal government guarantees on deposits/liabilities
- **Implicit support:** "Too big to fail" banks that the government is likely to rescue to prevent systemic collapse (even without a formal guarantee)
- **Analytical impact:** Government support can make a bank's debt safer than its standalone financial ratios would suggest. Conversely, withdrawal of government support (as has occurred post-2008) increases standalone risk.

> **Exam relevance:** If a question asks about factors that CAMELS fails to capture, government support is a valid answer.

---

#### 2. MISSION AND CORPORATE CULTURE

**Why it matters:** A bank's mission and culture shape its risk appetite, strategic priorities, and ethical standards.

- **Commercial vs. investment banking focus:** Commercial banking is generally lower risk; investment banking involves more trading and proprietary risk-taking
- **Growth-oriented vs. conservative culture:** Aggressive cultures may pursue higher-risk strategies for market share
- **Compliance culture:** Banks with weak compliance cultures face regulatory risk (fines, consent orders, criminal liability)

**From EOC Q13:** The supplementary information about XYZ Bank's "increasingly fierce competition" leading to "higher-risk strategies" is a negative cultural signal. The "net benefit plan obligation steadily decreasing" is a positive factor — lower defined benefit risk. "Above-average equity-based compensation" can align management interests but may also incentivize excessive risk-taking.

> **The correct answer for a favorable assessment** is declining net benefit plan obligation — it directly reduces the bank's financial risk.

---

#### 3. COMPETITIVE ENVIRONMENT

**Why it matters:** A bank's profitability and risk profile depend on its competitive position. This is **the most commonly tested CAMELS limitation.**

- Market share and pricing power
- Barriers to entry (regulatory, capital requirements)
- Competitive intensity (number and aggressiveness of competitors)
- Technology and fintech disruption

> **From EOC Q3:** "One limitation of the CAMELS approach is that it fails to address a bank's competitive environment." This is the **correct answer** — CAMELS covers sensitivity to market risk and management capabilities, but competitive environment is NOT directly addressed by any CAMELS component.

---

#### 4. OFF-BALANCE-SHEET ITEMS

**Why it matters:** Banks can have significant exposures that do not appear on the balance sheet but create real economic obligations.

Key off-balance-sheet items include:
- **Loan commitments** — irrevocable promises to lend that create contingent credit exposure
- **Letters of credit and guarantees** — obligations triggered by counterparty default or other events
- **Securitized assets** — loans sold to SPEs where the bank retains economic exposure (servicing, first-loss positions, liquidity facilities)
- **Derivative notional amounts** — the face value of derivative contracts (disclosed in notes, not on the balance sheet until mark-to-market creates an asset or liability)
- **Operating leases** (less relevant post-IFRS 16/ASC 842, which brought most leases on-balance-sheet)

> **Connection to derivatives:** At inception, many derivatives have zero asset/liability value and are only disclosed by their notional amount. As the market-to-market value changes, they appear on the balance sheet. The notional amount in the notes is often vastly larger than the balance sheet carrying value — understanding this distinction is critical.

---

#### 5. SEGMENT INFORMATION

**Why it matters:** Aggregated bank financials can mask enormous performance variation across business segments or geographies.

- A bank might have excellent US operations but deteriorating international operations (exactly the case with Citigroup's NIM analysis)
- Different segments have different risk profiles: retail banking vs. investment banking vs. wealth management vs. corporate banking
- Segment data reveals where earnings growth is coming from and which units are consuming capital disproportionately

**Citigroup example:** Overall NIM declined modestly from 2.90% to 2.86%, but this masked:
- US offices: NIM improved from 2.88% to 3.25% (strong)
- Non-US offices: NIM declined from 2.91% to 2.40% (significant deterioration)

Without segment analysis, the analyst would have concluded "roughly stable NIM" — completely missing the non-US deterioration.

---

#### 6. CURRENCY EXPOSURE

**Why it matters:** Internationally active banks face significant FX risk that can distort reported results.

- **Translation risk:** When foreign-currency assets and revenues are translated back to the reporting currency, a strengthening home currency reduces their reported value
- **Transaction risk:** Currency movements affect the economics of cross-border lending and borrowing
- **Impact on NIM:** Citigroup's declining non-US NIM was partly attributable to the strengthening US dollar, which created "negative foreign currency translation impacts"

> **Analyst assessment:** An analyst should evaluate whether management is adequately hedging currency risk or whether FX movements are creating persistent drag on returns.

---

#### 7. RISK DISCLOSURES

**Why it matters:** The quality and transparency of a bank's risk disclosures provide insight into the robustness of its risk management framework.

Key risk disclosures to evaluate:
- **Market risk** — VaR methodology, stress testing results, risk limits
- **Credit risk** — credit quality breakdowns, concentration limits, provisioning methodology
- **Operational risk** — cybersecurity, compliance, fraud prevention
- **Model risk** — reliance on internal models for risk weighting, hedging, derivatives pricing

> **Pillar 3 of Basel III** mandates extensive disclosure requirements to enable market discipline. Banks that exceed minimum disclosure requirements signal stronger risk management cultures.

---

|-------------------|----------------------------------------------|

### ③ IMPACT OF INTEREST RATE ENVIRONMENT

**This is heavily tested (EOC Q19, Q20) and connects to bank earnings:**

**How banks typically make money:**
- Borrow short-term (deposits) at low rates
- Lend long-term (mortgages, commercial loans) at higher rates
- Profit = the spread (NIM)

**Normal yield curve (upward-sloping):**
- Bank earns higher rates on long-term assets, pays lower rates on short-term deposits
- NIM is positive and healthy

**Inverted yield curve:**
- Short-term rates exceed long-term rates
- The bank's short-term funding costs rise while its long-term lending rates are locked in
- Net interest margin **decreases**

> **From EOC Q19:** If the yield curve is expected to invert, the net interest margin for banks that borrow short and lend long will most likely **decrease**. This is a core testable concept.

**Reverse repurchase agreements and liquidity:**
- A 30-day reverse repo is a collateralized loan that the bank makes — it gives cash and receives securities as collateral
- This is a **highly liquid asset** (matures in 30 days, fully collateralized)
- When a bank increases its reverse repo activity during periods of high retail deposit inflows, the ratio most directly affected is the **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)**, because reverse repos qualify as HQLA or near-HQLA for the 30-day liquidity calculation

> **From EOC Q20:** Reverse repos affect the LCR (short-term liquidity), NOT the NSFR (structural funding) or Tier 2 capital ratio.

---

### ④ TRADING REVENUE: QUALITY AND RISK ANALYSIS

**Key metrics:**

| Metric | Formula | What It Tells You |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Trading revenue / Total revenue | — | Percentage of revenue from volatile sources; higher = lower quality |
| Trading revenue / Average daily VaR | Annual trading revenue ÷ Average daily VaR | Reward-to-risk ratio; higher = better return per unit of risk |

**From EOC Q16:**
- The bank with the **lowest** trading revenue as % of total revenue has the **highest quality** earnings (because trading is volatile and unsustainable)
- A spike in trading revenue (e.g., 2009 crisis) does NOT mean high quality — it means the bank was taking outsized bets

**From EOC Q17:**
- Trading revenue / VaR improved at N-bank (134× → 160×) but was flat at T-bank (80× → 80×)
- N-bank improved its **reward-to-risk** for trading
- VaR is useful for **intra-company** trend analysis but not for **inter-company** comparison because calculation assumptions differ

## PART A: PROPERTY AND CASUALTY (P&C) INSURANCE

### ① THE BUSINESS MODEL

P&C insurers provide **risk management services** — for the price of a premium, they protect policyholders against losses that may be many times the premium paid.

**Revenue sources (from Travelers, Exhibit 27):**

| Source | 2016 % | Nature |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Premiums | 88.8% | Core underwriting revenue |
| Net investment income | 8.3% | Return on float (premiums collected but not yet paid as claims) |
| Other (fees, realized gains) | 2.9% | Minor |

> **Key insight:** The **float** — money collected as premiums and invested until claims are paid — creates a second revenue stream. This is why Warren Buffett calls insurance "a wonderful business if you can underwrite profitably, because you get paid to hold other people's money."

**Types of P&C insurance:**
- **Property insurance:** Damage to buildings, autos, environmental damage
- **Casualty (liability) insurance:** Legal liability to third parties for insured events
- **Multiple peril policies:** Cover both property and casualty from a single event (e.g., car accident with property damage AND passenger injuries)
- **Personal lines vs. commercial lines:** Consumer-facing vs. business-facing policies

**Distribution methods:**
- **Direct writers:** Own sales staff, internet sales, mail → higher fixed costs, lower variable costs
- **Agency writers:** Independent/exclusive agents, brokers → lower fixed costs, higher variable costs (commissions)

---

### ② THE UNDERWRITING CYCLE

P&C insurance is cyclical:

```
Hard Market → Premiums rise → Profits increase → New entrants attracted 
→ Competition intensifies → Price cutting begins → Soft Market 
→ Premiums fall → Profits decline → Weak players exit 
→ Underwriting standards tighten → Hard Market again
```

**What drives the cycle:** The combined ratio. When it's low (profitable), entrants flood in. When it's high (unprofitable), they leave.

---

### ③ THE FIVE P&C PROFITABILITY RATIOS

These are the **core testable formulas** for insurance analysis:

---

#### Ratio 1: Loss and Loss Adjustment Expense Ratio

$$\text{Loss Ratio} = \frac{\text{Loss Expense} + \text{Loss Adjustment Expense}}{\text{Net Premiums Earned}}$$

**Measures:** Quality of underwriting — how successful was the insurer in estimating the risks it insured?

**Lower = better.** A low ratio means implied losses were well-estimated and premiums were adequate for the risks assumed.

---

#### Ratio 2: Underwriting Expense Ratio

$$\text{Expense Ratio} = \frac{\text{Underwriting Expenses}}{\text{Net Premiums Written}}$$

**Measures:** Efficiency of money spent acquiring and managing underwriting business (commissions, salaries, marketing).

**Lower = better.** A low ratio means the insurer is efficient in obtaining new business.

> **Important note on denominator:** The loss ratio uses **net premiums earned** while the expense ratio uses **net premiums written**. Some companies (like Travelers) report a modified version using net premiums earned in both denominators for GAAP consistency. Be alert to this distinction.

---

#### Ratio 3: Combined Ratio

$$\text{Combined Ratio} = \text{Loss Ratio} + \text{Expense Ratio}$$

**Measures:** Overall efficiency of the underwriting operation.

| Combined Ratio | Interpretation |
|---------------|---------------|
| **< 100%** | Underwriting profit — the insurer is making money before investment income |
| **= 100%** | Break-even on underwriting |
| **> 100%** | Underwriting loss — the insurer is paying more in claims and expenses than it earns in premiums |

> **Critical exam point:** A combined ratio > 100% does NOT necessarily mean the insurer is unprofitable overall. Investment income on the float can offset underwriting losses. But a persistent combined ratio > 100% is a red flag.

---

#### Ratio 4: Dividends to Policyholders (Shareholders) Ratio

$$\text{Dividend Ratio} = \frac{\text{Dividends to Policyholders (Shareholders)}}{\text{Net Premiums Earned}}$$

**Measures:** Liquidity impact of dividend distributions relative to earned premiums.

---

#### Ratio 5: Combined Ratio After Dividends

$$\text{Combined Ratio After Dividends} = \text{Combined Ratio} + \text{Dividend Ratio}$$

**Measures:** A stricter efficiency measure that includes the cash outflow of dividends. Provides a fuller picture of total cash requirements.

---

### ④ APPLYING THE RATIOS — COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

**From Exhibit 29 (2016 data for selected P&C insurers):**

| Insurer | Loss Ratio | Expense Ratio | Combined Ratio | Dividend Ratio | Combined After Dividends |
|---------|-----------|--------------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Travelers** | 61.4% | 32.6% | **94.0%** | 3.1% | 97.1% |
| Hartford Financial | 82.2% | 48.8% | **131.0%** | 2.4% | 133.4% |
| W.R. Berkley | 61.1% | 37.3% | **98.4%** | 2.9% | 101.3% |
| CNA Financial | 76.1% | 39.9% | **116.0%** | 11.7% | 127.7% |
| **Markel** | 53.1% | 35.9% | **89.0%** | 0.0% | 89.0% |

**Key observations:**
- **Markel** has the best (lowest) combined ratio at 89% — efficient underwriting AND efficient operations
- **Hartford** is the worst at 131% — both the loss ratio (82.2%) AND expense ratio (48.8%) are the highest
- **Travelers** is median for loss ratio but below median (better) for expense and combined ratios → in the better-performing half

> **From EOC Q6:** The insurer with the most efficient underwriting operation is the one with the **lowest combined ratio**. This is Insurer C (97.0%) in the question, because it's the only one below 100%.

> **From EOC Q15:** When the loss ratio decreases (e.g., 61.3% → 59.1%), the insurer has **improved its ability to estimate insured risks** — not decreased overall efficiency (which would require the combined ratio to increase) and not improved premium-obtaining efficiency (which is the expense ratio, which actually increased).

### ⑤ LOSS RESERVES — THE CRITICAL ACCOUNTING JUDGMENT

Loss reserves are P&C insurers' **most material accounting estimate**. They represent the insurer's best estimate of future claims payments for events that have already occurred.

**Why they matter enormously:**
1. They drive pricing — underestimated reserves → underpriced premiums → future insolvency risk
2. They are subject to management discretion → potential for earnings manipulation
3. They become harder to estimate as the obligation extends further into the future (e.g., asbestos liabilities)

**Loss Reserve Roll-Forward Analysis (from Travelers, Exhibit 28):**

| Item | 2016 | 2015 | 2014 |
|------|------|------|------|
| Net reserves, beginning | $39,823M | $41,036M | $41,585M |
| + Current year claims estimate | 15,675 | 14,471 | 14,688 |
| – Prior year favorable revision | (680) | (817) | (885) |
| = Total increases | 14,995 | 13,654 | 13,803 |
| – Claims payments | (14,796) | (14,474) | (14,066) |
| +/– FX and other | (74) | (393) | (286) |
| Net reserves, ending | $39,948M | $39,823M | $41,036M |

**Key analytical observations:**

1. **Claims paid / Current year estimate:** $6,220M / $15,675M = 39.7% → most of the liability is short-term (paid within the year)

2. **Reinsurance coverage:** Travelers cedes 16.7–17.6% of gross reserves to reinsurers — significant risk transfer

3. **Prior year revisions as % of pretax income:**
   - 2016: $680M / $4,053M = **16.8%**
   - 2015: $817M / $4,740M = **17.2%**
   - 2014: $885M / $5,089M = **17.4%**

> **Critical insight:** Downward revisions of prior-year reserves boost current-year income. If these revisions consistently represent ~17% of pretax income, analysts must question whether:
> - The company is deliberately **over-reserving** initially to create a cushion for later release (conservative but potentially manipulative)
> - Or genuine estimation improvements are occurring

**Net premiums written vs. net premiums earned:**
- **Net premiums written** = direct premiums written, net of any premiums ceded to reinsurers
- **Net premiums earned** = the portion of written premiums earned over the relevant accounting period (premiums are billed in advance and earned over the coverage period)

## PART B: LIFE AND HEALTH (L&H) INSURANCE

### ⑧ THE BUSINESS MODEL

L&H insurers generate revenue from:
1. **Premiums** — from selling life and health insurance policies
2. **Investment income** — from investing collected premiums
3. **Fee income** — from investment products and services (annuities, unit-linked products)

**Key difference from P&C:** L&H policies are **long-term**, and claims are **more predictable** (based on actuarial mortality tables applied to large populations). This allows:
- **Lower capital requirements** than P&C (less cushion needed for unexpected losses)
- **Higher-risk investment strategies** (can seek higher returns from equities and real estate because the liability timeline is longer and more predictable)

**Types of L&H products:**
- **Term life:** Benefit paid if insured dies within fixed term; expires without value otherwise
- **Whole life / permanent:** Provides death benefit AND serves as a savings vehicle (cash value accumulation)
- **Annuities:** Fixed or variable payment products (often linked to market returns)
- **Health insurance:** Covers medical expenses or provides income during illness/injury

### ⑩ EARNINGS CHARACTERISTICS (L&H)

**Major expenses:**
- Policyholder benefits (death benefits, maturity payments)
- Contract surrenders (early cancellation → the insurer pays out accrued cash value and loses future premiums)
- Commissions and acquisition costs (capitalized and amortized; deferred acquisition costs, or DAC, is a significant balance sheet item)

**Areas requiring significant judgment:**
1. **Future policyholder benefit estimates** — based on actuarial assumptions (life expectancy, interest rates)
2. **Deferred acquisition costs** — amortized based on actual and expected future profits
3. **Securities valuation** — unrealized gains/losses on investment portfolios

**Profitability metrics:**
- ROA, ROE, growth/volatility of capital, book value per share
- Pre-tax and post-tax operating margins
- Pre-tax and post-tax operating ROA and ROE
- **Sector-specific (A.M. Best):** Total benefits paid as % of net premiums written + deposits; commissions and expenses as % of net premiums written + deposits

**Earnings distortion risk:**
- Asset-liability **valuation mismatch:** Assets at fair value, liabilities at historical cost → when interest rates change, assets move but liabilities don't, creating artificial P&L volatility
- This is why operating return metrics (which adjust for mark-to-market fluctuations) provide a better picture than GAAP net income

**From Exhibit 34 (MetLife vs US L&H sector):**
- MetLife's ROE was 12.2% in 2011 (above industry) but declined to 1.0% by 2016
- Its pretax operating ROE (9.0% → 7.5%) was more stable than GAAP ROE → confirming that GAAP ROE was distorted by non-operating items

### ⑫ LIQUIDITY (L&H)

L&H companies' liquidity needs are driven by:
- **Policyholder benefits** (death claims, maturity payments)
- **Contract surrenders** (early terminations → increasingly important as new products offer surrender options)
- Historically, liquidity was less critical because of long-duration liabilities
- Today, liquidity is more important because of newer products with early-exit features

**Liquidity measures:**
- Liquid assets (cash + marketable securities) relative to near-term liabilities
- Non-investment-grade bonds and equity real estate are less liquid
- Standard & Poor's uses a **liquidity model** comparing adjusted assets to adjusted obligations under both normal and stressed conditions
- The traditional "current ratio" is NOT directly applicable because insurance balance sheets don't classify items as current/non-current

---

### ⑬ CAPITALIZATION (L&H)

Like P&C, no global standard exists. Key differences in risk-based capital:
- **L&H claims are more predictable** → lower equity cushion needed → lower capital requirements
- **Interest rate risk is material** for L&H because of long-duration liabilities → L&H risk-based capital formulas **incorporate interest rate risk** (unlike P&C, whose capital formula focuses on underwriting and asset risk)

### ⑭ P&C vs L&H — THE KEY COMPARISON TABLE

| Feature | P&C | L&H |
|---------|-----|-----|
| **Policy duration** | Short (annual) | Long (multi-decade) |
| **Claims** | Variable, unpredictable | Predictable (mortality-based) |
| **Capital requirements** | **Higher** | **Lower** |
| **Investment style** | Conservative (86% fixed income, 1% equities) | More aggressive (73% FI, 20% equities) |
| **Key profitability metric** | Combined ratio | Operating ROE / ROA |
| **Key liability concern** | Loss reserves | Future policyholder benefits |
| **Interest rate sensitivity** | Moderate | **High** (long-duration liabilities) |
| **Liquidity needs** | High (unpredictable claims) | Historically lower, increasing |

This is **backwards** — it is L&H insurers who have lower capital requirements and can seek higher returns, precisely because their claims are more predictable.
